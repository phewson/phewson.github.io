COVID: Mathematical models and statistical models

COVID: Mathematical models and statistical models

Thursday, 6 Aug, 2020 By Paul Hewson.

One thing the COVID-19 pandemic has achieved is an incredible and widespread interest in numerical descriptions of the outbreak. You can’t go far on social media without encountering an armchair expert on R numbers. Sometimes they call them R-nought but I’m sure they mean R(t). Now we can find errors in their understanding and scoff, or we can use this as an educational opportunity. People are now interested in data, but they are also interested in models. I would suggest that one big misunderstanding is the difference between mathematical and statistical models. I believe there is an important difference between the two, but it is very hard to specify exactly. And how would you explain it to a member of the public who has just developed an interest in policy predictions from a particular model. More importantly, in the context of Teaching Statistics, how do you explain this to a learner of statistics? I would go further. I would argue that one thing that has been missing from many of the mathematical models has been an ongoing and systematic criticism of those models using standard statistical techniques. Also, some very accessible data activities can lead to a deeper insight.

The first statistical issue is that we need to be clear what we are measuring

  • Someone who had a test and died within a specified time window of that test
  • Someone who died and was assessed as having COVID
  • Excess Mortality

Similarly with tests

  • Antibody test indicating that someone has had an infection
  • PCR test - how much RNA is indicative of an infection?
  • The difference between testing symptomatic “patients” and a randomly selected member of the public

Hospitalisation:

  • Intensive care

None of these lead to clear cut answers. One step is to ensure consistency over time.

I suggest that modelling is directed towards predicting what might happen in the future; either to extrapolate the existing trend or to suggest what might happen in response to various policy initiatives.

We can model these data using a simple extension to a linear model. We include cubic terms, i.e., model death count as a function of time, time^2 and time^3. The other technical alteration is that we have fitted the model using generalised least squares in order to allow for the non-independent errors. The blue lines seem to fit the data failry well, although it is clear they don’t quite capture the peak of the epidemic in late April.

Now, we can see what this “best fit” model does if we try to predict the future. It’s widely known that extrapolation from a linear regression is risky, but some authorities regard cubic terms in regression models as an abuse of regression. It’s little surprise that we get crazy predictions, but we can see that the “forecast” is for deaths off the scale very quickly.

Finally, we superimpose the actual counts. We can clearly see that the forecasts were badly wrong.

Now, it will surprise no-one that a regression model including a cubic term and extrapolating beyond the range of the data has predicted so badly. There are other statistical models we could use to try to find a better fit, but none of them fix the prediction problem.

Maybe a logistic growth model is enough to illustrate the key ideas. We have a population that (at time \(t\) is denoted as \(P\), and a maximum possible population size (the carrying capacity) \(K\). Essentially this says that the rate of growth is equal to:

\(\frac{dP}{dt} = rP\left( 1 - \frac{P}{K} \right)\)

where \(r\) is the growth rate. The idea is that represents some fundamental state of nature. If we know \(K\) and \(P(t)\) at any given time, we can estimate \(r\) or indeed forecast \(P(t)\) for some future time point.

The so-called SIR model (Susceptible, Infected, Recovered) is an extension of this model, where we not only have \(K\), the carrying capacity but rather we have \(S\) the number of peple susceptible and \(R\) the number of people recovered (or dead) and the change in the number of people infected depends on both of these. Now, the maths of this model go beyond the scope of Teaching Statistics, but are very nicely set out in Convergence. The idea is that we develop a model of the rate of change in the number of people who are infected given that the number of people in the population who are susceptible and the the number who have recovered (or died) also changes all the time.

Hypothetical SIR model

Now the point is that the SIR model is not fit to data. There are parameters (the transmission rate, the number of people who are infected by a given person), the recovery rate and the ratio of the birth rate to the death rate. There are also initial values for the number of Susceptible, Infected and Recovered people. But given these we are forecasting from the initial values. This is the simplest possible model, we also need to account for the proportion who die, the demographics (different susceptibility/infectivity at different ages) and different mortality by age and sex. And then we need to add in mathematics that describes the effect of different policy interventions, such as vaccination.
